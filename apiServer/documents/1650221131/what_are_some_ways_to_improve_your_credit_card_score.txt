2. What affects your credit score? 


Your credit score is a three-digit number that lenders use to help them decide how risky it is to lend you money. The higher your credit score, the less risky it is for a lender to lend you money, and the lower your interest rate is likely to be.

Your credit score is based on information in your credit report. Your credit report includes information about how you've used credit in the past, such as how much you've borrowed and whether you've paid your bills on time.

Your credit score is also affected by your credit utilization ratio. This is the percentage of your available credit that you're using. For example, if you have a credit card with a $1,000 limit and you've already borrowed $500, your credit utilization ratio is 50%.

Your credit score is also affected by the length of your credit history. The longer you've had credit, the better your credit score is likely to be.
